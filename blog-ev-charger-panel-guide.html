Does Your Home Need a Panel Upgrade Before Installing an EV Charger?

Short answer: it depends on your panel's amperage and what else is already drawing power. Here's how to check before you buy a charger.

Start with your panel's amperage

Most homes built before the 1990s have a 100-amp panel, which was fine for the appliance load of that era. A Level 2 EV charger draws roughly 30–50 amps on its own circuit, which can push an older 100-amp panel past what it can safely support once you add AC, an electric dryer, and everything else already running.

What a load calculation tells you

Before quoting an EV charger install, we run a load calculation — adding up the demand from your existing circuits against your panel's rated capacity. If there's enough headroom, we install the charger circuit directly. If not, a panel upgrade to 200 amps is usually the fix, and it's a one-time cost that also covers you for future additions like a generator or workshop.

What this actually costs

A straightforward EV charger install with available panel capacity typically starts around $650. If a panel upgrade is needed first, expect to budget for both — the upgrade starts around $2,400, and we can often schedule both in the same visit.

How to check before calling anyone

Open your panel and look at the main breaker at the top — it's usually labeled with the amperage (100, 150, or 200). That number alone won't tell you if you have room for a charger, but it's the first thing any electrician will ask, so having it ready speeds up your quote.
